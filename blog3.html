Good Design

Some Recent Thoughts

I've been working on a Flappy Bird-inspired game called Batsy to familiarize myself with Godot, and it's been a great learning experience. Godot’s scene system and scripting language, GDScript, made implementing mechanics like procedural obstacles and item pooling relatively easy. Compared to other engines I've used, I found that scaling elements in Godot is much smoother, which was a big plus when setting up my game’s layout. While there were a few quirks to get used to, like signal management and node-based organization, overall, the workflow felt intuitive, and I was able to iterate quickly.

For Batsy, I wanted to ensure a high-quality experience with a clean layout, smooth movement, and rewarding mechanics. The game isn’t finished yet, but I estimate it will be ready in a few more days. One of the cool features I implemented is object pooling for coins and rocks to optimize performance, so instead of constantly creating and destroying objects, the game reuses them when needed. This prevents unnecessary lag and improves efficiency, especially as the game speeds up. I also tweaked the spawning logic to ensure a fair but challenging experience—coins appear in varied, non-repetitive patterns, and obstacles are spaced intelligently to avoid impossible scenarios.

gif shows incomplete game

Another interesting addition was dynamic difficulty scaling—the game gradually increases in speed, forcing players to react faster as they progress. To make the world feel more alive, I included smooth scrolling ground and randomized rock placements, ensuring no two playthroughs feel exactly the same. Collecting coins adds to a persistent total, which I plan to expand with unlockable skins or power-ups in the future. With just a few more refinements, Batsy will be polished and ready to go, and I’m excited to see how it turns out!
